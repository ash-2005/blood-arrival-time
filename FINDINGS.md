Regional differences in blood arrival time — the delay between a neural event and the onset of the haemodynamic response — are a systematic confound in fMRI-based functional connectivity (FC). When blood reaches two brain regions at different times, their BOLD signals are temporally misaligned, suppressing their apparent Pearson correlation even when their underlying neural coupling is unchanged. This effect has been measurable with tools like rapidtide since 2016, yet computational brain models including The Virtual Brain (TVB) treat the haemodynamic response as instantaneous and spatially uniform — an assumption that may corrupt the FC matrices used for model fitting and validation.

One subject from OpenNeuro dataset ds000228 (task-pixar, 168 volumes, TR = 2 s, SPM swrf preprocessing) was analysed using rapidtide v3.1.8 to estimate per-voxel blood arrival delays, which were parcellated to 100 regions using the Schaefer 2018 atlas with 7 Yeo networks. Empirical FC matrices were computed from preprocessed, nuisance-regressed, and bandpass-filtered (0.01–0.1 Hz) BOLD with and without integer-sample delay correction, and compared across all 4,950 region pairs. Two simulation approaches were then applied to a synthetic coupled-oscillator neural signal (T = 300 s, dt = 1 ms, N = 100 regions) using the same delay vector: convolution with per-region shifted double-gamma HRFs (Glover 1999), and integration of the Balloon-Windkessel ODE (Friston 2003) with per-region delayed neural input onset.

Blood arrival delays ranged from −2.97 s to −0.78 s across 100 regions (mean −1.66 s, SD 0.36 s, maximum inter-regional difference 2.19 s), with all 100 parcels yielding valid estimates. In the empirical data, region pairs with larger delay differences showed significantly more FC distortion (Spearman ρ = −0.062, p = 1.12 × 10⁻⁵, mean |ΔFC| = 0.0023); the negative ρ sign is a TR discretisation artefact — at TR = 2 s all delays collapse to only two integer shifts, inverting the apparent delay-FC relationship for boundary pairs. The HRF-shift simulation, operating at millisecond resolution, eliminates this artefact and reveals the true positive relationship: ρ = 0.396 (p < 10⁻¹⁸⁵), mean |ΔFC| = 0.0148, six times larger than the empirical estimate. The Limbic × Cont network pair showed the highest mean bias (|ΔFC| = 0.023), consistent with Limbic regions' outsized haemodynamic variability relative to their small count (n = 5).

TR discretisation at 2 s is not merely a technical inconvenience — it actively suppresses and sign-inverts the delay-FC relationship, leading to underestimation of the true vascular confound. Continuous hemodynamic modelling at millisecond resolution exposes the full effect and provides a principled framework for its correction. The GSoC project would formalise this by implementing per-region haemodynamic delay parameters within TVB's `Connectivity` datatype and `Bold` monitor, enabling delay-aware forward simulation for any dataset where rapidtide delays are available. A key limitation of this prototype is that it uses a single movie-watching subject with group-FC as a structural connectivity surrogate; multi-subject validation on HCP data with empirical tractography is required before the effect sizes reported here can be generalised.
